Bayes Plurinacional

Science is a human institution that aims to reach truths, intersubjective agreements with universal validity. The formal sciences (mathematics, logic) reach these agreements by deriving theorems within closed axiomatic systems. {However, the empirical sciences (from physics to the social sciences) must validate their propositions within open systems that always contain some degree of uncertainty. Is it then possible to reach intersubjective agreements ("truths") in the empirical sciences if it is inevitable to say ``I don't know''? Yes. In short, we can avoid lying: not say more than what is known while incorporating all the available information.
For example, if we know that there is a gift behind one of three boxes, what can we say? Where is the gift? If we put all our belief in one of the boxes, we are saying more than we know. We don't have certainty that the gift is in that box. Since there are no reasons to prefer any particular box, in the end, we will agree to divide the belief equally, one-third for each box. By maximizing uncertainty (dividing equally) given the available information (among the three boxes), we reach an initial intersubjective agreement in contexts of uncertainty! But now, how do we preserve intersubjective agreement in more complex cases when we receive new information?
The rules of probability have been known since the late 18th century, and they have been adopted as a reasoning system in all empirical sciences. They are conceptually intuitive: preserving the prior belief that remains compatible with the data (product rule) and predicting with the contribution of all hypotheses (sum rule). The problem was the computational cost, as it required evaluating each and every hypothesis. Therefore, over time, partial solutions were developed: statistical physics in the 19th century and the frequentist approach in the 20th century. However, it was not possible to apply this reasoning strictly until the late 20th century, when there was a tremendous growth in computing power.
Despite the fact that under uncertainty, conclusions are "plural and conditional," the traditional language of empirical sciences has been "singular and definitive." Just as selecting a single hypothesis has known negative consequences in probability (overfitting), the imposition of a single type of society has had increasingly evident negative consequences. Despite all the advances, metropolitan science was not able to compensate for the loss of millenary knowledge caused by colonial-modernity, and as a consequence of the massive loss of global cultural diversity, we are currently experiencing a serious ecological crisis that continues to deepen.
Currently, the growth of the Bayesian approach is a global phenomenon that is accelerating. Latin America has also begun this transition, albeit at a slower pace. In this context, a group of specialists has decided to organize the Plurinational Bayesian Community. The word "Plurinational" holds a very special meaning in South America. It represents the coexistence of our local cultures. This vision is particularly well suited to a Bayesian community because, like probabilistic reasoning that believes at the same time in mutually contradictory hypotheses, a plurinational society adapts through the coexistence of our diversities.
